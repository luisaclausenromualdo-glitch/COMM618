**What the data says about gun deaths in the U.S.**

Although this is not necessarily an article from a news outlet, I found it could be helpful to several journalists. This Pew Research Center data analysis of CDC, FBI, and other sources found roughly 44,000 Americans died from gun-related injuries in 2024, the fifth-highest total on record, though declining for the third straight year. It found gun homicides have fallen sharply since a pandemic-era peak in 2021, while gun suicides are at near-record highs. The analysis also found gun death rates vary by state, and while the U.S. leads the world in absolute gun deaths, several Latin American nations have higher per-capita rates.
One of the reasons I like this piece is because it draws on CDC data for deaths, FBI data for weapon types and mass shootings, and IHME data, which covers 204 countries and territories, and it's transparent about which source is being used for what. It’s interesting to look at different methodologies and coverage.
I found it helpful that each time the article shows how the numbers of U.S. gun deaths have changed over time, tracing back to 1968, which gives a complete idea of how this has evolved over time. The charts are clean and easy to understand. 
Obviously this is a data brief and not a story and Pew is transparent that this is a research summary. As a journalist, I read this and thought “What would be my angle here? How could I add human voices? How would I explain why trends are moving the way they are? Why are gun suicides rising while homicides fall?” And many other questions. I believe this could serve as a great data story idea generator on an incredibly important topic.
