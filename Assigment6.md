**A Widespread Problem: Patterns of Obstetric Violence in Ghana Among 1,854 Women** 

A research published in Frontiers in Public Health Education and Promotion from 2023 surveyed  1,854 women who gave birth in eight public health facilities in the Ashanti and Western Regions of Ghana and revealed a strikingly high prevalence of obstetric violence. 
Approximately two in three women (65.3%) reported experiencing at least one form of obstetric violence during childbirth. The horizontal bar chart illustrates the distribution of specific types of mistreatment, highlighting that non-confidential care and abandoned care were the most commonly reported forms, affecting 35.8% and 33.4% of women, respectively. 
Non-dignified care (28.5%) and physical abuse (27.4%) were also prevalent, whereas non-consented care (7.5%), detention for inability to pay for care (7.7%), and discriminated care (11.0%) were less frequent but remained significant.
These patterns underscore that women’s experiences of obstetric violence are not limited to isolated incidents. 
For instance, a woman who experienced non-dignified care may also have been subjected to physical abuse or abandoned care, emphasizing the cumulative burden of mistreatment. 
These percentages represent the total sample, not just women who reported obstetric violence, which reinforces the scope of the problem across the population.
The dataset is quite robust. It is based on a facility-based, cross-sectional survey using structured questionnaires, covering a large sample of women aged 15–45.
The research is extensive and has a lot more data. There are more charts that could be done from the information. 
Beyond the numbers, the chart tells a broader story about systemic challenges in obstetric care in Ghana. High rates of non-confidential and abandoned care point to organizational and cultural factors that place women at risk of trauma, complications and decreased trust in facility-based deliveries. 
These insights suggest that interventions should focus not only on individual behaviors but also on institutional practices and norms that perpetuate obstetric violence, highlighting the urgent need for policies and training that protect women’s rights during childbirth.
