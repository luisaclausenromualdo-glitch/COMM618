**Gen Z religious revival claim withdrawn over ‘flawed’ YouGov survey**

The (London)Times published an article exposing the "quiet revival" report and its sginificant data failure of what became a widely circulated story about Gen Z's return to religion in the UK.
The Bible's Society report claimed a surge in church attendance among young people in 2024, but YouGov, which conducted the surveys, but this month admitted that key quality control systems were "not activated" because of human error. The error allowed fradulent, overseas and repeat respondents to skew the results. 
YouGov took full responsability and issued an apology, while the Bible Society, which had repeatedly sought and received assurances about the data's reliability, said it was "deeply dissapointed."

This article caught my attention because it was centered around a flawed data collection and it showed how it can mislead institutions that rely on it. It's also interesting to see the influence the study had on churches and religious organizations that had a stake in the findings being true.
The error was in basic methodoly failure and it went undetected for a while, especially considering academics had been raising red flags for months. 
The article displayed a couple of simple graphics that fitted the story well and its findings. However, the piece could have been tigher and avoided certain repetitions. 
The most interesting thing about this is that rather than reporting on a data-backed claim, it reports on the unravelling of one, and holding both the pollster and the commisioning organization to account is the kind of story many readers may appreciate.
