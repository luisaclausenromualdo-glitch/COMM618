**	Immigrants at Camp East Montana face medical neglect in a county where finding enough doctors is a challenge.**


When Immigration and Customs Enforcement arrested “Frank” in August, he had one request: to have his insulin. 
ICE  took him to Camp East Montana, the largest immigration detention facility in the country, located in El Paso, Texas. In a sworn declaration to the American Civil Liberties Union,  submitted under a pseudonym, “Frank,” who is diabetic, said his medical requests were ignored at least ten times, leading to 15 days without his insulin or his blood sugar and blood pressure pills. 
“Frank” was not alone. ACLU and a coalition of nonprofits interviewed more than 45 detained people as part of a letter sent to ICE and lawmakers who shared their accounts of medical neglect, denial of meaningful access to counsel, hunger, insufficient food and more. “Maria,” a transgender woman detained at the same facility, said she was denied her prescribed antidepressants for more than a week after her arrival. And “Xavier,” who suffers from kidney stones, described the process of requesting medical attention as a “negotiation.” 
Federal government data shows Camp East Montana was built in a county formally identified as unable to meet its own residents’ basic healthcare needs. Then, the federal government awarded the facility’s medical contract to Loyal Source Government Services, a company with a documented record of neglect spanning more than a decade. An analysis of federal data, contract records and a 200-page Senate Judiciary Committee investigation shows the sickest people detained at Camp East Montana are being held in a county with not enough doctors to go around, according to data from Health Professional Shortage Area, which identifies regions and low-income populations where medical resources are most strained.
Camp East Montana is near communities that government data has designated as unable to attract enough doctors and nurses to serve the people living there. Data from as recent as March shows a gap in primary care doctors in several parts of El Paso County. For example, in areas like the northeast corner of the county, 21 physicians would need to appear tomorrow to provide the minimum standard of care. El Paso County has 19 active federal health professional shortage designations across primary care and mental health, according to the HRSA. In plain, the data shows the county cannot recruit enough physicians, nurses and mental health professionals to serve the people who already live there. That is the workforce pool from which Loyal Source was expected to staff a medical operation at Camp East Montana and the stories of medical shortage and medical neglect show the issues inside the detention mirror some of the challenges of its own county. 
“Xavier” said he waited days in level-10 pain before resorting to asking his boyfriend outside the facility to call 911 on his behalf. Another detainee, “Hernan,” shared in the declaration that he twisted his ankle in the yard and was unable to walk. He said he requested medical care every single day after the injury, going so far as putting his name on the “sick list” at least 20 times, but received no medical attention. 
Charlotte Weiss, an attorney with the Texas Civil Rights Project who has visited Camp East Montana every week since the fall, said each visit has revealed similar complaints from the people detained: ignored medical requests, delayed care and a facility where basic hygiene standards were neglected. Some of the consequences became visible in February, when a measles outbreak swept through the facility, with at least 14 confirmed cases and 112 people placed in isolation, leading to a two-week lockdown. Weiss said she wasn’t surprised. 
“This was the foreseeable and tragic outcome for a facility that has neglected and abdicated its responsibility to those in its custody for months,” Weiss said.
The warnings about Loyal Source did not begin with Camp East Montana. 
In 2025, the Senate Judiciary Committee released an investigation that began in 2023 and documented a pattern of failure from Loyal Source at other facilities where the company held medical contractors.
The investigation followed the death of Anadith Reyes Alvarez, an 8-year-old girl who died due to medical neglect in 2023 at the Harlingen Border Patrol Station in Cameron County, Texas, another federally designated healthcare shortage area. Cameron County mirrors El Paso County as a county the federal government has identified as medically underserved. HRSA data shows the county is part of a three-county mental health shortage area covering Cameron, Hidalgo and Willacy, which are missing nearly 35 full-time mental health providers.
In Reyes Alvarez’s case, she had sickle cell disease and a congenital heart condition, which were documented in the facility’s electronic medical records system upon her arrival. However, Loyal Source medical staff who were treating her in the days leading up to her death never accessed her or reviewed her history, according to the investigation.

Under its contract, Loyal Source was required to have a medical provider present for nearly every scheduled shift at the facilities it staffed. In June 2022, the investigation found the company left more than a third of those shifts uncovered, meaning detained people seeking care arrived at medical stations with no one there to provide care. Troy Hendrickson, a CBP veteran who turned whistleblower regarding Loyal Source, alleged that the company provided unsafe care, lacked qualified staff and overbilled for services, contributing to negligent medical care at border facilities. A staff member from the facility reported to the investigation that at times they were the only provider responsible for 200 to 300 arriving migrants in a single shift. The Senate’s report called the company’s overall performance “catastrophically inadequate.”

The Senate investigation concluded that placing people with complex chronic conditions in areas where local healthcare infrastructure is too thin to support emergency transfers creates a systemic weakness that will continue to produce preventable harm. 
Kassandra Gonzalez, a senior attorney with the Texas Civil Rights Project, represents the families of two children who died in CBP custody in the Rio Grande Valley after not receiving proper medical care. Gonzalez said Loyal Source has a long history of medical negligence and patterns of death in other CBP facilities in Texas, and if they continue to get awarded contracts, the same pattern will continue. 
“I couldn’t believe it when I heard they were the contractor at Fort Bliss,” Gonzalez said of the Camp East Montana facility. “Yet, it also doesn’t shock me. And unfortunately, more deaths have followed.”

Obtained health professional shortage area data from HRSA and HPSA. The tools displayed only whole-county geographic designations. It identified sub-county and population-specific shortage designations. In El Paso’s case, I filtered from January 2025 until today and in Cameron County’s case, I filtered from Jan. 1, 2019, to the present because I wanted to know the situation of the county when the deaths at CBP detentions started. 
Federal contract was obtained from usaspending.gov. I used it to compare how much money Loyal Source has been awarded in the years after the deaths and the investigation.
